Two Nations, Two Markets

An analysis of the U.S. and Canadian luxury jewelry sectors reveals fundamental differences that challenge unified marketing strategies and demand a more sophisticated approach to media modeling.

The Core Finding & Recommendation

The U.S. and Canadian luxury markets are not interchangeable. A simplistic, pooled-data approach to measure media impact will produce flawed results. The strategic path forward requires a more nuanced model.

Primary Finding

The markets differ profoundly in scale, growth, consumer values, and competitive dynamics. A single strategy for North America is suboptimal.

Proposed Solution

Adopt a Hierarchical (Multilevel) Marketing Mix Model to accurately capture country-specific nuances and deliver reliable ROI insights.

The Modeling Dilemma: Apples & Oranges

The core problem with a unified model lies in its foundational assumptions. The geographic units are not comparable, and the model incorrectly assumes that marketing effectiveness is constant across these profoundly different markets.

ðŸ‡ºðŸ‡¸ U.S. Designated Market Area (DMA)

Defined By: The Nielsen Company (Private)

Basis: Television Viewing Habits

Concept: A "Media-Shed" - a region that consumes the same local TV content.

Purpose: Media planning and ad buying.

ðŸ‡¨ðŸ‡¦ Canadian Census Metro Area (CMA)

Defined By: Statistics Canada (Government)

Basis: Commuting & Economic Flows

Concept: A "Commuter-Shed" - a functional economic region.

Purpose: Socio-economic analysis.

Conclusion: DMAs and CMAs are methodologically incompatible as interchangeable units in a single-level panel model.

The Pitfall of "Pooling" Data

A simple pooled model averages the distinct market realities, creating a single, misleading result. It masks the higher ROI in Canada's growth market and the nuanced performance across diverse U.S. regions.

The Path Forward: Hierarchical Modeling

The recommended solution is a Hierarchical (or Multilevel) Marketing Mix Model. This advanced approach is designed for nested data, allowing it to respect market differences while producing stable, reliable estimates of media effectiveness.

1

Model Country Effects

Estimates a specific ROI adjustment for Canada vs. the U.S., quantifying systemic differences.

2

Model Regional Effects

Allows ROI to vary across different DMAs and CMAs, identifying top-performing regions.

3

"Borrow Strength"

Uses data from all markets to improve the reliability of estimates for any single market, a process called "partial pooling."

Strategic Business Impact

This approach enables smarter budget allocation, tailored media strategies for each country, and a true, defensible measure of marketing ROI across North America.
